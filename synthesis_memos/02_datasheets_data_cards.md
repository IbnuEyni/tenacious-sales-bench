# Synthesis Memo: Datasheets for Datasets & Data Cards

**Papers:** Gebru et al., 2021; Pushkarna et al., FAccT 2022
**Category:** Common (all paths)
**Date:** 2026-04-28

## Core Contribution

Gebru et al. propose a standardized 7-section documentation template for datasets: motivation, composition, collection process, preprocessing, uses, distribution, and maintenance. Pushkarna et al. extend this with layered detail — telescopic (one-liner), periscopic (paragraph), microscopic (full detail) — making documentation navigable for different audiences.

## Key Design Choices Relevant to Tenacious-Bench

1. **Collection process transparency** (Gebru Section 3.3): Document who collected the data, what mechanisms were used, and whether consent was obtained.
2. **Intended and prohibited uses** (Gebru Section 3.5): Explicitly state what the dataset should and should not be used for.
3. **Layered detail** (Pushkarna Section 4): Telescopic for quick scanning, microscopic for reproducibility audits.

## Where I Disagree: The "Collection Process" Section Assumes Human-Sourced Data

Both frameworks were designed for datasets collected from or about people — survey responses, social media posts, medical records. Gebru's Section 3.3 asks: "Who was involved in the data collection process? How were they compensated? Was consent obtained?"

These questions don't map cleanly to Tenacious-Bench. Our dataset is:
- **33% programmatic** — generated by parameter sweeps over structured slots. No human "collected" these; a script produced them deterministically.
- **30% trace-derived** — restructured from our own agent's outputs. The "subjects" are synthetic prospects, not real people.
- **25% multi-LLM synthesis** — generated by LLMs, filtered by other LLMs. The "collection process" is a software pipeline.
- **12% hand-authored** — the only portion with a human author.

**Evidence from Week 10:**
- Trace `out_enrichment_complete_c0033ad8_4` shows segment classification of a synthetic company "Consolety" as "n/a." This trace is our data — we generated it, we own it, there's no consent question. But Gebru's framework would have us document consent for data we created ourselves.
- The Crunchbase ODM sample (1,001 companies) is the only external data source. It's publicly available under ODM license. But our tasks don't contain raw Crunchbase records — they contain *synthetic scenarios inspired by* the kinds of companies in Crunchbase.

**My position:** For predominantly synthetic benchmarks, Section 3.3 ("Collection Process") should be replaced with a **Generation Process** section documenting: (a) which models generated which tasks, (b) the filtering pipeline and rejection rates, (c) the seed-to-task amplification ratio, and (d) the contamination prevention protocol. This is more useful for reproducibility than consent documentation for data that has no human subjects.

**Design decision:** Our `datasheet.md` will follow Gebru's 7 sections but replace Section 3.3's human-collection questions with generation-process documentation. We adopt Pushkarna's layered detail throughout — telescopic in README, periscopic in the datasheet body, microscopic in `generation_scripts/` and `contamination_check.json`.

## What I Adopt

- All 7 Gebru sections as the structural backbone — the framework is sound even when individual questions need adaptation.
- Pushkarna's telescopic/periscopic/microscopic layering — essential for a benchmark that must be usable by strangers in under an hour.
- Explicit "Intended Uses" and "Prohibited Uses" sections — Tenacious-Bench evaluates B2B sales agents specifically; using it to evaluate general chatbots would produce meaningless scores.
